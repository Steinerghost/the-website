Oregon State Seed Lab

Seed Lab Purity Technical Assistant

Jun 2016 - Mar 2018 (1 Yr. 9 Mo.)

Skills Practiced

  • Attention to Detail
  • Priority Management

Responsibilities

  • Mix and screen seed samples according to ASOA and ISTA standards
  • Clean mixing and screening equipment in between each sample
  • Complete sample completion goals based on daily/weekly sample count
  • Maintain clean work environment that is free of possible contaminants

Relevant Links

  • Seed Lab Home Page
  • Recommendation Letter

Description

Assisting with laboratory duties in line with Association of Official Seed Analysts (AOSA) and International Seed Testing Association (ISTA) regulations. Days started by accepting seed samples from various farming companies both locally and internationally, giving the team an idea of the workload/type for the shift. Individuals would be either mixing or screening the seed samples under the above regulations in order to prepare them for the seed analysts to examine. Each person on the team would process at least 40 samples per day. The equipment used would have to be cleaned in between each sample to prevent contaimination. This was the hardest part of the job, as one seed left behind could contaminate an entire sample. These seeds could be as small as a pinhead, and can float through the air. These aspects of the job fostered strong attention to detail due to the cleaning processes, as well as priority management by ensuring the vast variety of sample types were processed efficiently given the possible equipment/talent bottlenecks.

Environmental Engineering Internship

Jan 2014 - Sep 2014 (8 Mo.)

Skills Practiced

  • Good Laboratory Practices
  • Communication to Multiple Audience Types
  • Compilation of Data into Meaningful Results in Excel

Responsibilities

  • Create and manage anaerobic batch reactors to mimic conditions in wastewater treatment plants
  • Sample batch reactors for methane production and pH levels
  • Detail all procedures and data in a hand written lab book
  • Compile data in excel to see trends and statistical significance within experiments
  • Report results to peers, mentors, and the public

Relevant Links

  • Johnson Internship
  • Dr. Tyler Radniecki
  • Poster

Description

For my environmental engineering internship, I worked with Dr. Tyler Radniecki in his environmental engineering lab. I worked with him during the school year, and then was able to persue the opporunity fulltime due to the Johnson Internship. My project was titled "The Effects of Heavy Metals on Anaerobic Co-Digesters". This project was based on optimizing methane production at wastewater treatment plants (WWTP). Microbes within WWTP can consume organic material, and create methane. This methane can then be used for energy. The ultimate goal is to make WWTPs self sufficient using this methodology. My project looked at how heavy metal contaminants commonly found in wastewater would affect methane production. My day to day tasks revolved around managing my own batch reactors which modeled WWTP reservois, and maintaining the lab through cleaning and material preparation. I would assemble/disassemble batch reactors using sludge samples obtained from the Corvallis WWTP, and vary levels of common heavy metals and organic materials. Routines involved measuring levels of gas output and reactor characteristics such as organic solid levels and pH. Lab maintenence revolved around ensuring courteous lab usage, such as replacing used buffers, cleaning glassware, and disposing of hazardous materials. I would attend weekly lab meeting to discuss progress and possible next steps. My internship ended with a presentation poster being presented at an engineering symposium to incoming underclassmen.

Description

The volunteer work acted as an outreach program for children K-12. The goal was to get kids excited about pursuing a career in a STEM field. To accomplish this, a group of volunteers would visit schools throughout the area and perform science demonstrations in front of the children and parents. These demonstrations were held in a open house fashion, where groups could come and go as they please to visit other booths. Typically, the experiments we would run would be reverse engineering diapers, creating gels beads in solution, and operating a balance controlled segway. These experiments helped me practice public speaking to a diverse audience. Technical information varied in complexity depending if I was talking to an adult, highschooler, or 3rd grader. Working with these kids also give me a great sense of pride hoping that I helped shape someones life in the sciences. Watching children have fun with making gel worms and brag to their parents and peers showed they were engaged, and that we accomplished our mission.
